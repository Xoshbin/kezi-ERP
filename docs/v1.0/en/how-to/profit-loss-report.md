# Profit & Loss Report: Your Business Performance

This guide explains how to use the **Profit & Loss** (or Income Statement) to understand your company's profitability over a specific period. If the Balance Sheet is your financial "snapshot," the Profit & Loss is your "video" spanning a range of dates.

---

## What is a Profit & Loss Report?

The Profit & Loss (P&L) report summarizes your **revenues**, **costs**, and **expenses** during a specific period. It answers the fundamental question: *"Did we make money or lose money this month?"*

It is built on this simple equation:

> **Income - Expenses = Net Profit (or Loss)**

**Why does this matter?**
1.  **Profitability**: Shows if your core business is sustainable.
2.  **Expense Control**: Highlights where you are spending the most.
3.  **Tax Preparation**: This is the primary report used to calculate income taxes.

---

## Where to Find It

Navigate to: **Accounting → Reports → Profit & Loss**

> **💡 Tip**: You can also find it under the "Reporting" section of your dashboard or reports hub.

---

## How to Use the Report

### 1. Set Your Date Range
Unlike the Balance Sheet (which is "as of" one date), the P&L needs a **Start Date** and an **End Date**.

*   **This Month**: `Jan 1` to `Jan 31`
*   **Last Quarter**: `Oct 1` to `Dec 31`
*   **Year to Date (YTD)**: `Jan 1` to `Today`

### 2. Generate the Report
Click the **Generate Report** button (<heroicon-o-play class="w-4 h-4 inline"/>).

## Understanding the Sections

The report follows a logical flow from top (Income) to bottom (Net Profit).

### 1. Operating Income (Revenue)
Money earned from your core business activities.
*   **Sales**: Income from selling goods or services.
*   **Service Revenue**: Fees earned from consulting or labor.

### 2. Cost of Revenue (COGS)
The direct costs related to producing the goods or services you sold.
*   *Example*: If you sell coffee, this is the cost of the beans and milk.
*   *Equation*: **Gross Profit = Income - Cost of Revenue**.

### 3. Operating Expenses
The indirect costs of running the business (overhead).
*   **Salaries**: Payroll for administrative staff.
*   **Rent & Utilities**: Office space, electricity, internet.
*   **Marketing**: Ads, promotions.
*   **Depreciation**: The gradual expense of your assets over time.

### 4. Net Profit / Net Loss
The "Bottom Line." This is what remains after ALL expenses are subtracted from your total income.

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Net Profit is not the same as Cash Flow.**
> You might show a profit but have no cash if your customers haven't paid their invoices yet. Check the **Cash Flow Statement** for that detail.

---

## Interpreting the Numbers

### Gross Profit Margin
This tells you how efficient your core business is.
*   *Formula*: `(Gross Profit / Total Revenue) * 100`
*   *Goal*: Higher is better. It means you keep more money from every sale before paying for overhead.

### Net Profit vs. Net Loss
*   **Positive Number**: You made a profit! 🎉
*   **Negative Number**: You operated at a loss. This is normal for new businesses but concerning for established ones.

---

## Common Scenarios

### Scenario A: High Revenue, Low Profit?
**Problem**: You sold a record amount ("Great sales!"), but your Net Profit is near zero.
**Investigation**:
1.  Look at the **Operating Expenses** section.
2.  Did you spend too much on **Marketing** or **Travel**?
3.  Are your **COGS** increasing? Maybe suppliers raised prices but you didn't raise yours.

### Scenario B: Comparing Performance
**Goal**: See if you are doing better than last year.
1.  Run the report for **This Quarter** (e.g., Q1 2024).
2.  Run the report for **Last Year, Same Quarter** (e.g., Q1 2023).
3.  Compare the **Net Profit** line.

---

## Best Practices

### 📅 Timing
*   **Accrual Basis**: Record income when you invoice the customer, not when they pay. This gives a truer picture of performance.
*   **Monthly Review**: Check your P&L every month-end to catch spending issues early.

### 🔍 Drilling Down
*   **Clickable Rows**: Most modern reports allow you to click on a line (like "Office Supplies") to see the individual bills that make up that number. Use this to find errors or surprise huge expenses.
